Robert Moses, the most significant character in NYC historical signs

by Hongyu Liu

April 3, 2023


“…the jaundiced eye of the city historian sees no signs of achievement and progress. He is obsessed with the harlotry and the decline and fall of Rome and Babylon…” Robert Moses (1888-1981) wrote for the Atlantic in Jan. 1962.


Starting his career in the NYC municipal government in 1913, Moses' over five-decades-long career brought significant change to the look of the city. Best known for his position as the New York City Parks Commissioner, Moses is arguably one of the most influential characters in the development of this megapolis.

Robert Moses with Battery Bridge model. Source: Wikimedia Commons

Parks Tycoon


“It is simply not possible to spend more than a few hours in New York without being exposed to the vast legacy of this latter-day Trajan,” Thomas J. Campanella commented in his article for Bloomberg.


Despite never being an elected official, Moses left traces of his works across the five boroughs.


The NYC Department of Parks and Recreation erects historical signs in parks “honors worthy people” of the city’s past. Among the over two thousand pieces of historical signs across the city, one in seven has spared at least a phrase mentioning Moses' influence on the region. The number eclipses his successor Henry J. Stern for 100 mentions, far from the reach of other historical figures including George Washington.

Such a dominant can be partially attributed to his attention to building new recreational facilities, where most of these historical signs lie. The NYC park acreage doubled from 1934 to 1960 when he was in office. His other achievements included the addition of 658 playgrounds and 17 miles of beach.

Many of the signs credited him for designing the highway system in New York City, one of his most influential yet controversial projects throughout his career.


Since the 1930s, Moses has led the development of 416 miles of highway and thirteen bridges in the city. The historical sign on the Robert Moses Playground commented that he “[brought] New York into the automobile era.”

Robert Moses’ Career through historical signs

The Brooklyn-Queens Expressway (BQE) was first proposed in 1936 as a part of the plan to improve NYC transportation. In 1940, Mosese wrote a detailed proposal to expand the scale of the project. The Expressway reformed the look of the city, hence a trail of historical signs mentioning Moses can be found along the BQE.

Brooklyn Bridge park next to the Brooklyn Heights. Photo by Hongyu Liu

A huge cluster of signs mentioning Moses can be found around Brooklyn Heights, the most distinctive section of the Expressway. Under the resident's petition to minimize the expressway's disturbance to the existing neighborhood, Moses designed the expressway as a three-story structure.

The demolition of buildings from this construction resulted in a scatter of small parks that mentions Moses, proof of his role in shaping the neighborhood as we know it today.

Department of Parks and Recreation wrote in their historical sign in the Marcy Green South that:” Combining his roles with the TBTA and Parks, Moses created a series of small parks to beautify and buffer the area around the busy expressway. While most of the expressway parks are simply green traffic islands and sitting areas, a few, like this one, which provides basketball and handball courts, provide recreation space as well.”

The original plan of the Grand Central Parkway came to shape in the 1860s. Landscape architect Frederick Law Olmsted (1822-1903). Moses lobbied for the construction of the Grand Central Parkway in 1930 and received funding from the New Deal.

The Flushing Meadows Corona Park was designed for the 1939-40 World's Fair. The site was reused to host the 1964-65 World's Fair. Moses was hired as the designer of the site then, hence a large number of historical signs mention his name.
